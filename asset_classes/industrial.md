# Industrial — Underwriting Nuances

## Revenue model
- **NNN-dominant** — tenant pays RE tax, insurance, CAM directly or via reimbursement
- **Lease-by-lease** — rent/SF NNN, escalations (typically 2.5–4%/yr fixed), term (5–15 yrs typical)
- **Mark-to-market** — industrial rents have grown 40–80% in many markets 2020–2024; in-place often well below market — major source of value at rollover
- **Free rent / TI** — minimal for warehouse re-tenanting; meaningful for first-gen or build-to-suit
- **Recoveries** — typically 100% of OpEx + RE tax recoverable

## Building specs that matter
- **Clear height** — modern logistics requires 32–40 ft; older buildings (24–28 ft) are functionally obsolete for big-box users
- **Dock doors** — ratio of doors per 10K SF (target ~1:10K for distribution)
- **Trailer parking** — increasingly important; constrains certain users
- **Bay depth** — wider bays (50–60 ft) more efficient
- **Power** — kVA capacity (relevant for cold storage, light manufacturing)
- **Sprinkler density** (ESFR, K-25.2) — required for racked storage

## Sub-types (very different fundamentals)
- **Distribution / logistics** — large box (>200K SF), regional/national logistics
- **Last-mile** — smaller (50–150K SF), close to dense population, e-commerce delivery
- **Light industrial / flex** — 10–50K SF, mixed warehouse/office, local users
- **Cold storage** — specialized; high CapEx, sticky tenants, premium rents
- **Manufacturing** — heavy power, specialized; usually owner-occupied
- **Truck terminal / IOS** — outdoor storage; growing institutional interest

## OpEx specifics (largely tenant-paid under NNN)
- RE tax + insurance (recoverable)
- CAM (recoverable)
- Capital reserves — minimal for warehouse ($0.10–0.20/SF/yr); heavier for cold storage

## Asset-class-specific DD
- Tenant credit (D&B, public filings, parent guarantee)
- Lease assignability + use restrictions
- Environmental Phase I (industrial use raises Phase II likelihood)
- Floor slab condition + load capacity
- Roof age + warranty
- Truck court depth (130 ft = modern standard for large box)
- Zoning permits for current use; future use flexibility

## Submarket data points to track
- Rent growth (Y/Y) — industrial has been the highest-growth sector
- Vacancy — historically low; rising in some markets due to construction pipeline
- Net absorption
- Construction pipeline + scheduled deliveries
- Top user moves (Amazon footprint changes are bellwether)
- Distance to major distribution nodes (port, intermodal, interstate)
- 3PL vs. user concentration

## Data sources
- CoStar Industrial — rent, vacancy, supply, transactions
- Port volume data (e.g., Port of LA/Long Beach for SoCal)
- CBRE / Cushman / JLL industrial reports (free)
- BLS QCEW — transportation & warehousing employment
